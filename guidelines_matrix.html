About Practice Guidelines

What Are Practice Guidelines?

Practice guidelines are systematically developed statements designed to assist clinicians and institutions in making decisions about appropriate care for specific clinical situations. Unlike primary research studies, guidelines synthesize existing evidence and expert consensus into actionable, graded recommendations. They are produced by professional societies, specialty organizations, and multidisciplinary expert panels, and are intended to serve as a reference standard for institutional policy and clinical decision-making.

Four formal practice guidelines are included in this critical appraisal, each using a different grading system to express the strength and quality of their recommendations. Bouza et al. (2021) use the GRADE/SIGN system, rating evidence quality from 1++ (highest) to 4 (lowest) and recommendation strength as Strong or Conditional. Brocki et al. (2010) apply Oxford Centre for Evidence-Based Medicine (CEBM) grades, ranging from Grade A (strongest) to Grade D (expert opinion only). Grant et al. (2024) rate evidence quality as High, Moderate, or Low. Lazar et al. (2016) use the ACC/AHA classification system, with Class I indicating strong evidence for benefit, Class IIa indicating probable benefit, Class IIb indicating possible benefit, and Class III indicating no benefit or potential harm, each paired with a Level of Evidence (A, B, or C).

The matrices below present these recommendations in two formats: first, a focused matrix isolating only those recommendations that directly address external sternal support devices; second, a comprehensive matrix spanning all ten clinical outcome domains addressed across the four guidelines.

Sternal Binder Recommendations

Guideline Recommendations Specific to External Sternal Support Devices

Of the four included guidelines, three contain explicit recommendations pertaining to the use of external sternal support devices — including rigid thoracic vests (e.g., Posthorax), corsets (e.g., Stern-E-Fix), and supportive brassieres for women with macromastia. The matrix below isolates only those recommendations, presented across the outcomes for which sternal binder use is directly addressed: sternal wound dehiscence, deep sternal wound infection (DSWI), hospital length of stay, and activity and sternal precautions.

Lazar et al. (2016) assign external chest support vests a Class IIb / Level of Evidence B recommendation for DSWI, sternal dehiscence, and hospital LOS — indicating possible benefit based on non-randomized evidence, while noting that vests are cumbersome and patient compliance post-discharge may be poor. Bouza et al. (2021) issue a Strong recommendation supporting postoperative sternal immobilisation systems in all patients, graded at evidence level 2+. Brocki et al. (2010) recommend supportive vests for patients with BMI ≥35 and frequent coughers (Grade D), and a supportive brassiere for women with bra cup size ≥D (Grade B). Grant et al. (2024) do not make a specific sternal binder recommendation.
