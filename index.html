Project Overview

Background, rationale, and scope of this comprehensive review.

Median sternotomy remains the standard surgical approach for the majority of cardiac procedures, including coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) and valve surgery. While advances in surgical technique and perioperative care have reduced complication rates, sternal wound complications — including dehiscence, superficial and deep sternal wound infection (SSWI/DSWI), and mediastinitis — continue to carry significant morbidity and mortality, with deep infections associated with mortality rates of up to 47% in some series and hospital costs exceeding $62,000 per case.

External sternal support devices (ESSDs), including rigid sternal vests (e.g., the Posthorax vest) and adjustable braces (e.g., the QualiBreath brace), have been proposed as a means of reducing post-sternotomy complications by limiting sternal mobility, reducing lateral traction forces, supporting wound healing, and potentially decreasing pain during coughing and mobilization.

Despite their clinical use and a growing body of primary literature, the evidence base for ESSDs remains heterogeneous and methodologically varied. This project provides a comprehensive, critically appraised synthesis of the available evidence — spanning randomized controlled trials, systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and formal practice guidelines — with particular attention to methodological quality, population generalizability, and clinical significance beyond statistical significance.

All included studies are evaluated using validated appraisal tools (Cochrane RoB 2, AMSTAR 2), and effect size calculations (Cohen's d, phi, RR, ARR, NNT) are independently computed to augment reported p-values with clinically actionable metrics.

Clinical Significance
Why Sternal Complications Matter
DSWI occurs in 1–4% of cardiac surgery cases but carries mortality up to 47%, prolongs hospitalization, and is no longer reimbursed by CMS following CABG — creating both clinical and institutional imperatives for effective prevention.
Devices Under Review
Posthorax Vest & QualiBreath Brace
The Posthorax vest (Epple Inc., Vienna) applies anteroposterior stabilization with lateral cushions to limit sternal separation. The QualiBreath brace (EC Medica) is an adjustable device designed to be titrated to patient tolerance. Both are critically distinguished throughout this review.
Methodological Note
Critical Appraisal Embedded
Several included studies carry identified limitations: industry sponsorship (Hjorth 2014), wrong population (El-Ansary 2008, 2015), absence of device use in intervention arm (Katijjahbe 2018). These are noted explicitly in all synthesis outputs.
Grading Framework
GRADE with Independent Effect Sizes
Outcome certainty is assessed using GRADE. Effect sizes (Cohen's d, phi, RR, ARR, NNT) are independently calculated for all primary outcomes to provide clinical interpretive value beyond reported p-values.

PICO Framework

The structured clinical question guiding this literature review with critical appraisal.

P Population Adult patients undergoing median sternotomy for cardiac surgery, including coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG), valve procedures, and combined operations. Studies limited to chronic sternal instability populations or non-sternotomy incisions are excluded.
I Intervention Application of an external sternal support device (ESSD) in the postoperative period, including rigid sternal vests (Posthorax), adjustable sternal braces (QualiBreath), or equivalent external chest stabilization devices worn following sternal closure.
C Comparator Standard postoperative care without an external sternal support device, or standard elastic thoracic bandage/compression dressing. Studies must include a comparator arm; single-arm observational studies are excluded.
O Outcomes Primary: Deep sternal wound infection (DSWI), sternal wound dehiscence. Secondary: Superficial sternal wound infection (SSWI), mediastinitis, hospital length of stay, sternal instability severity (SIS grading), pain scores, pulmonary function, quality of life, and mortality.
