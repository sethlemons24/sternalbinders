2023 – 2024

Initial Literature Review

In 2023–2024, a literature review was conducted to evaluate whether external sternal support devices should be incorporated as part of a care bundle aimed at reducing sternal surgical site infections following cardiac surgery. The review examined six articles spanning a range of study designs, including white papers, case studies, randomized controlled trials, literature reviews, and perspective papers.

The articles were selected to characterize the existing evidence on external sternal binders — including the QualiBreath adjustable brace, Posthorax vest, Stern-E-Fix (SEF), and HeartHugger — and their association with sternal wound complications, pain, physical function, and quality of life.

Article Summaries — Initial Review
Author, Year, Journal, Study Type Population Group Outcomes Key Results Comments
Hjorth, D. (2014)
Qualiteam White Paper
White Paper 		Sternotomy patients (general postoperative population); synthesis of multiple published studies Sternal wound infection (DSWI & SSWI); pulmonary complications; persistent postoperative pain; external chest support device effectiveness; health care costs DSWI incidence: 0.3–7.3% at 90 days; additional cost $41,500–$88,800/case. PPC incidence: 8–79%; additional cost ~$28,700/case. Persistent pain: 11–56%; additional cost $4,500–$7,700/case. For 1,000 annual sternotomies: ~$2.5M (DSWI), ~$12.5M (PPC), ~$2.0M (pain). QualiBreath reduced sternal gap by 20% vs. no support. Posthorax vest significantly reduced DSWI across 3 trials (refusal rates 24–31%). QualiBreath highest-rated device on all self-report measures. Industry-sponsored (Qualiteam). No original data. Concludes QualiBreath is superior. Notes comfort and patient adherence essential for home use. Criticizes heart-shaped pillows as infection risk and ineffective.
El-Ansary et al. (2015)
Int J Therapy & Rehabilitation
Case Study 		Single 75-year-old male, 9 months post 3rd median sternotomy for coronary revascularization. Comorbidities: obesity (BMI 37), T2DM, COPD, hypertension, prior stroke with right hemiparesis. Presenting with SIS grade 3 (complete sternal instability). Sternal instability (SIS grade 0–4); pain with activity and at rest (NRS 0–10); functional recovery / ADLs; sleep quality; sternal healing (real-time ultrasound) With QualiBreath brace: pain reduced from 7/10 to 2/10 with activity. Uninterrupted sleep; improved confidence with everyday tasks. At 2-year follow-up: SIS grade 3 → 1. Ultrasound confirmed 8mm sternal gap persisted but with fibrous connective tissue union. Single case report; no control group; limited generalizability. Treatment commenced 9 months post-surgery. Demonstrates interdisciplinary approach. Ultrasound highlighted as valid, non-invasive monitoring tool.
Katijjahbe et al. (2018)
Journal of Physiotherapy (SMART Trial)
RCT 		72 adults post cardiac surgery via median sternotomy (CABG, valve, or combined). Mean age 63–64 years; predominantly male (86–94%); mean BMI ~30–31. Two metropolitan hospitals, Melbourne, Australia. Primary: physical function (SPPB). Secondary: upper limb function; hand grip strength; pain (NRS); kinesiophobia (Tampa Scale-11); health-related QoL (SF-36); sternal stability (Modified SIS); adherence; adverse events No significant between-group difference in SPPB at 4 weeks (MD 1.0) or 12 weeks (MD 0.4). Both groups improved significantly over time. Sternal instability: 1 patient per group (3%) at Weeks 4 and 12. Deep sternal wound complications: 1 per group. Adherence: 81% (experimental) vs. 70% (control). First RCT comparing standard vs. modified sternal precautions. No external chest support device was part of either intervention. Study likely underpowered; ceiling effect on SPPB by Week 4. Concludes modified precautions equally safe and appropriate.
Brocki et al. (2010)
Eur J Cardiovascular Nursing
Literature Review 		No single patient population; literature review of adult cardiac surgery patients post median sternotomy. Search: CINAHL, PubMed, Cochrane, PEDro; January 1992–June 2008. Mechanical stress factors causing sternal dehiscence, instability, incisional pain, and wound infection. Evidence base for activity precautions: coughing; obesity; loaded arm movements; skin stress; macromastia; supine-to-sit transfers. Coughing: greatest single stressor; intrathoracic pressure rises to 300 mmHg. No evidence supports weight restriction if arms kept close to body within pain-free range. Vest recommended for frequent coughers, BMI ≥35, bra cup ≥D. Self-hugging during cough protective; elbow method for transfers recommended. Skin healing: ~10 days; sternal bone healing: 28–35 days. Evidence levels range 3b–5 (low); most recommendations Grade D. Macromastia recommendation Grade B. No original data collected. Concludes precautions should focus on possibilities, not restrictions. Antonovsky's SOC framework used to support patient-centered approach.
El-Ansary et al. (2008)
Arch Physical Medicine & Rehabilitation
Cross-Sectional Randomized 		15 patients with chronic sternal instability ≥8 weeks post median sternotomy (12 men, 3 women). Age: 49–80 years. All scored ≥2 on SIS. Comorbidities: obesity, diabetes, COPD, osteoporosis. Sternal separation (mm) by real-time ultrasound during functional tasks and arm elevation. Self-report (100mm VAS): comfort, pain attenuation, feeling of support, ease of upper-limb movement, ease of breathing. Across 4 conditions: no support, sports tape, compression garment, adjustable brace. Sternal separation: No support 13.6mm | Sports tape 13.4mm | Compression garment 11.9mm | QualiBreath brace 11.0mm. Brace vs. no support: −2.7mm (~20% reduction, p<0.04). Self-report composite mean: No support 5.5 | Brace 8.4. Brace rated highest on all 5 VAS subscales. 13/15 subjects would have chosen a device immediately post-surgery. Small sample (n=15); chronic instability only; no acute group. Within-subject randomized order design. Sports tape caused more discomfort than no support. All 3 women preferred a support bra over any tested device.
El-Ansary et al. (2019)
Physical Therapy (PTJ)
Perspective Paper 		No single study population; narrative review and perspective synthesis. Target population: adults undergoing cardiac surgery via median sternotomy (>1 million procedures/year worldwide). Sternal complications and risk factors; impact of surgery on symptoms, physical activity, and QoL; clinical application and rationale of sternal precautions; influence of upper body functional tasks on sternal forces; proposal of clinical paradigm shift toward early upper body activity Sternal complication incidence: 1–8% worldwide; unchanged over 2 decades. Persistent post-sternotomy pain: incidence 21–66%. Arm muscle mass reduced by 55% at 1 year post-surgery. Common symptoms: fatigue (83%), difficulty sleeping (56%), shoulder pain (54%), weakness (50%). SP rationale lacks robust RCT support. QualiBreath brace significantly reduced sternal separation and improved pain in chronic instability. Perspective paper; no original data collected. Multi-institutional author group. Proposes individualized, progressive, activity-based rehabilitation. Recommends early screening for sternal risk factors and SIS use at discharge and outpatient rehab.

2026

Updated Critical Appraisal & PICO Review

In 2026, the original articles and conclusions were revisited as part of an updated literature review and critical appraisal. The PICO criteria were formally defined and applied retrospectively to the six prior analysis articles, resulting in the exclusion of four studies that fell outside the updated eligibility criteria.

These exclusions were made strictly on the basis of pre-specified PICO criteria and were not made on the basis of study quality. Two articles — Brocki et al. (2010) and El-Ansary et al. (2019) — met the updated criteria and were carried forward into the current critical appraisal.

Footnote — Excluded Studies

Four studies are displayed with grey overlays indicating exclusion from the primary evidence synthesis. These studies were excluded based on pre-specified PICO criteria and were not excluded on the basis of study quality.

Hjorth (2014): Excluded on the basis of study design. This document is an industry-sponsored white paper produced by the manufacturer of the QualiBreath device and was not subjected to independent peer review. It does not constitute primary research or an independent systematic review.

El-Ansary et al. (2008) and El-Ansary et al. (2015): Excluded on the basis of population. Both studies examined patients with chronic sternal instability — a population presenting with persistent, established instability months to years after the initial surgical procedure. This falls outside the target population of this review, which focuses on the acute and subacute post-surgical period. The findings of these studies remain clinically valuable within their own context and have been described narratively in this review.

Katijjahbe et al. (2018): Excluded on the basis of intervention. This randomized controlled trial compared standard versus modified sternal precautions (activity restrictions) and did not include the use of an external sternal support device in either group. It is an important study within the sternal precautions literature and its findings are discussed in the narrative context of this review.
